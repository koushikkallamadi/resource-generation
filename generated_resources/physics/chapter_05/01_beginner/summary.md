To understand how scientists study the tiny structures of materials, we use "Characterization Techniques." Think of these as super-powered microscopes and scanners that help us "see" the invisible.

The most important ideas are:

1.  **X-Ray Diffraction:** Scientists bounce X-rays off a sample. By looking at how the rays scatter (based on $$n\lambda = 2d \sin \theta$$), they can figure out how atoms are arranged, like solving a 3D puzzle.
2.  **Microscopy:** Techniques like SEM or AFM physically scan a surface with a tiny probe or electron beam to create a map of the surface, much like how you feel a surface with your fingers to tell if it is rough or smooth.
3.  **Thermal Analysis:** Methods like TGA/DSC measure how a material changes as it gets hot, helping us identify what it is made of by watching how it melts or loses weight.

| Technique | Goal |
| :--- | :--- |
| **X-Ray Diffraction** | Maps the atomic structure |
| **Microscopy** | Takes pictures of surfaces |
| **Thermal Analysis** | Measures changes during heating |
