### **Part 2: Short Answer Questions (2–3 Marks)**

1. **State Bragg’s Law of Diffraction.**
   *Model Answer:* Bragg’s Law is given by the equation: $n\lambda = 2d \sin\theta$. Here, $n$ is an integer, $\lambda$ is the wavelength of incident X-rays, $d$ is the spacing between crystal planes, and $\theta$ is the angle of incidence.

2. **Why are electron microscopes preferred over light microscopes for high-resolution imaging?**
   *Model Answer:* Electrons have significantly shorter wavelengths than visible light. Since resolution is inversely proportional to wavelength, electron microscopes achieve much higher resolution, allowing the visualization of structures at the nanometer scale.

3. **Differentiate between crystalline and amorphous substances based on X-ray diffraction patterns.**
   *Model Answer:* Crystalline substances exhibit long-range order and produce sharp, well-defined Bragg peaks. Amorphous substances lack long-range order, causing X-rays to scatter in many directions, resulting in a broad "hump" rather than sharp peaks.

4. **What is the function of the "goniometer" in an X-ray diffractometer?**
   *Model Answer:* The goniometer is the mechanical instrument used to maintain the specific geometry of the experiment; it rotates the sample at angle $\theta$ and the detector at $2\theta$ to ensure Bragg conditions are met.

5. **Define the Tapping Mode in AFM.**
   *Model Answer:* In Tapping Mode, the cantilever oscillates near its resonance frequency. The image is formed by detecting changes in the oscillation amplitude caused by intermittent contact forces between the tip and the sample surface.

6. **What is the primary purpose of TGA (Thermogravimetric Analysis)?**
   *Model Answer:* TGA monitors the change in mass of a substance as a function of temperature. It is used to determine thermal stability, decomposition mechanisms, and the presence of volatile components.

7. **Explain the role of the photomultiplier tube in UV-Vis spectroscopy.**
   *Model Answer:* It is a highly sensitive detector that converts incident light into an electrical signal. A single photon hitting the cathode generates a cascade of electrons, which are collected at the anode, resulting in an amplified measurable current.

8. **Mention two applications of Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM).**
   *Model Answer:* (i) Visualizing the internal structures of viruses and bacteria. (ii) Studying nanoparticles like ZnO to analyze their morphology and size distribution.

9. **Why is a vacuum required in an Electron Microscope?**
   *Model Answer:* A vacuum prevents electrons from colliding with air molecules, which would scatter the beam and disrupt the formation of a clear, focused image.

10. **Explain the "feedback system" in Scanning Probe Microscopy.**
    *Model Answer:* The feedback system maintains a constant interaction parameter ($P = P_0$) between the tip and sample. If the sample topography varies, the system detects a change ($\Delta P$) and uses a piezo transducer to adjust the gap, keeping the probe at a constant distance.

### **Part 3: Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)**

1. **Describe the working principle of a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) with the help of its components.**
   *Outline:* Mention the electron source (tungsten filament), electromagnetic lenses for focusing, the scanning coil for raster scanning, the interaction that produces secondary electrons, and the detector which converts these electrons into an image on a display.

2. **Explain the Rietveld method in XRD analysis.**
   *Outline:* Define it as a least-squares procedure that minimizes the sum of squared differences: $S_y = \sum w_i (Y_i - Y_{ci})^2$. Explain that it calculates the entire powder pattern to refine parameters like atomic positions and lattice constants.

3. **Discuss the principle and instrumentation of UV-Vis-NIR Spectroscopy.**
   *Outline:* Focus on the Beer-Lambert law ($A = \epsilon lc$), the light source (Tungsten/Deuterium lamps), the monochromator for wavelength selection, the cuvette (quartz), and the detector (photomultiplier/diode array).

4. **Compare TGA, DTA, and DSC.**
   *Outline:* Create a table comparing these thermal methods. TGA measures mass vs. temperature; DTA measures temperature difference ($\Delta T$) vs. temperature; DSC measures heat flow differences to identify endothermic/exothermic phase changes.

5. **Elaborate on the "Preparation of specimen for visualization by TEM."**
   *Outline:* Steps: (i) Chemical fixation (e.g., glutaraldehyde), (ii) Dehydration (ethanol), (iii) Embedding in epoxy plastic, (iv) Ultramicrotomy to create 20–100nm slices, (v) Staining with heavy metals (lead citrate) to increase electron scattering contrast.

### **Part 4: HOTS Questions**

1. **If an experimenter finds a "broad bump" at low 2θ angles in an XRD scan instead of sharp peaks, what can be concluded about the sample, and why?**
   *Reasoning:* The sample is amorphous. Diffraction requires periodic, ordered planes (long-range order) for constructive interference. Without this order, interference is not directional, creating a wide-range scattered bump.

2. **Why does the resolution of an AFM decrease as the tip becomes blunt?**
   *Reasoning:* The resolution in SPM/AFM is defined by the geometry of the tip's apex. A sharp tip interacts with a localized area (atomic scale), whereas a blunt tip averages the interaction over a larger area, losing fine surface detail.

3. **In DSC, why must the temperature of the reference and sample be maintained at a nearly constant level while the temperature program is running?**
   *Reasoning:* DSC measures the energy required to maintain this isothermal or controlled-ramp state. If the sample undergoes a phase transition (like melting), it absorbs or releases heat; the instrument must compensate by providing or withdrawing energy to keep the reference and sample temperatures equal, which allows for the measurement of the enthalpy of the transition ($\Delta H = KA$).
